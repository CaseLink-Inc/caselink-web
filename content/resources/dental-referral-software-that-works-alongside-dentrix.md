"Will it work with Dentrix" is the question that kills most dental software before the demo ends. For referral software, the honest answer is that it never touches Dentrix at all, and that is exactly why it works.

Dentrix is one of the most widely used practice management systems in dentistry. It runs scheduling, charting, billing, imaging, and patient records. The staff knows it cold. The workflows are built around it. Anything that asks an office to replace Dentrix or learn a parallel clinical system is finished before it starts.

Referral management is a job Dentrix was never built to do. A GP practice on Dentrix can note that a referral was recommended. Once the referral leaves the office, Dentrix has no way to track whether the specialist received it, whether the patient called, whether a consult was booked, or whether treatment happened. The referral drops into a gap the system was never designed to see across. That gap is where dedicated referral software fits, and it does not require touching scheduling, charting, or billing to fill it.

## What Dentrix does well, and where it stops

Inside the four walls of a practice, Dentrix handles nearly everything. Patient records and charting. Scheduling and confirmations. Insurance and billing. Treatment planning. Imaging. Reporting on production and collections.

Where it stops is the office boundary. When a GP refers a patient to an endodontist, a periodontist, or an oral surgeon, Dentrix can record the recommendation and print or fax a form. After that, visibility ends. The GP's front desk cannot see whether the specialist got the referral. The specialist has no shared view of the patient's referral history. Follow-up means phone calls, and those calls lose every time against the patient standing at the check-in window.

This is not a flaw in Dentrix. It was built to manage one practice. Coordinating a referral between two or more practices is a different kind of problem that needs a shared system connecting offices around a patient who is moving between them.

## A practice management system and a referral platform are two different jobs

Confusing the two leads to one of two bad outcomes. Either you adopt a platform that tries to replace the PMS, which is expensive and disruptive and rarely works, or you expect the PMS to do a job it was never designed for.

A **practice management system** manages what happens inside one practice. Records, scheduling, billing, notes, imaging. Dentrix, Eaglesoft, and Open Dental all live here.

A **referral platform** manages what happens between two practices. The referral itself, the handoff, the shared timeline, the messaging about a specific patient, and the outcome that tells both sides whether the case was completed.

They are complementary layers. The PMS does not need referral features. The referral platform does not duplicate charting or billing. Each does its job and the practice runs both.

For a Dentrix office, the day-to-day is simple. The front desk creates a referral on the platform when the dentist recommends a specialist. The case carries patient identifiers, the clinical reason, and any relevant files, including radiographs exported from Dentrix and clinical photos. The specialist receives it in a structured inbox. Both offices share a timeline through defined stages. At no point does anyone stop using Dentrix.

## What to evaluate when you already run Dentrix

Four things matter most.

**No forced migration.** It should work alongside Dentrix. If the pitch requires replacing the PMS, the real cost includes retraining, data migration, and disruption, which is rarely worth it for a referral workflow.

**HIPAA alignment.** The platform handles patient information, so it needs end-to-end encryption, audit logging, and a Business Associate Agreement. No BAA, no identifiable patient data.

**File support.** Radiographs, photos, and notes have to travel with the referral. A platform that sends only a name and a phone number has not improved on the paper slip.

**Shared visibility.** The GP should see what the specialist sees. When the patient calls, both offices know. When treatment is accepted or declined, both see it. That is what kills the follow-up call.
